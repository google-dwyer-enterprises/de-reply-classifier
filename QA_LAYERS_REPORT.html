3What the new Q/A layers added

The trigger was the reseller concern from the review call — the old AI gate judged companies from a marketing blurb and never saw any website. The fix became a general verification system:

❌ Before

  • Brand-vs-reseller judged from company name + marketing description only
  • Nothing ever visited a website
  • No second opinion on any verdict; no evidence trail
  • Size, country, ownership taken on faith from scraped data

✅ After

  • Every company's website is read — catalog, branding, language
  • Suspicious verdicts get an independent second check (ownership search, web footprint)
  • Every verdict carries a quoted piece of evidence — "why was this accepted?" always has an answer
  • Ambiguity goes to a human review queue with evidence attached — never auto-passed
FREE

Layer 1 — Hard evidence, no AI

Store catalogs read directly (who makes each product?); names matched against 275,000 registered Amazon brands (trademark-backed). Decides ~half of all companies deterministically.

~1¢ / company

Layer 2 — Website read + AI judgment

Homepage fetched; AI judges from actual page content and must quote its evidence. Unsure escalates, never passes silently.

~3¢ / company

Layer 3 — Web research

Blocked sites judged from their public footprint (LinkedIn, Amazon, press). Reseller-looking catalogs get an ownership check — are those vendor names other companies or this company's own product lines?

HUMAN

Layer 4 — Review queue

Anything still ambiguous appears in the review page with evidence attached. Nothing ambiguous is ever auto-decided.
Measured before it shipped. Tested twice against 60 known resellers + the full accepted pool before going live: zero false reseller flags by the AI site-check across both runs, 75% catch on judged known resellers — and most "misses" were companies the old filter had wrongly rejected (BDI Furniture, Butler Specialty, Diamond Cosmetics are real brands it threw away).

4a. Criterion 1 — brand vs reseller: clean after cleanup

The Q/A layers found and removed 5 verified resellers (AIRE, norda, RENOUN, SwiftPaws, Wildsmith — each confirmed by the ownership check: their catalogs are majority verified-independent brands). Just as important, 4 companies a cruder check would have wrongly rejected were saved — Vetnique, Montana Knife Company, Fisk Industries, Tropical Seas all look like resellers in raw vendor data, but those "vendors" are their own product lines. The independent audit then re-accused two more companies, and both accusations were overturned by hands-on browser inspection (cheerleading.com is a 60-year custom-uniform manufacturer — exactly your "manufacturers are fine" rule). Final state: zero known resellers in the accepted pool, and every judgment-based verdict double-checked with 92/92 agreement.

4b. Criteria 2, 6, 7, 8 — categories, emails, domains, dedup: zero violations

The audit found no banned-category leads (no cannabis/alcohol/firearms/adult), no generic inboxes, no bad domains, no duplicates, no contact-cap breaches. The scrape-time filters for these criteria are doing their job.

Bottom line: of the nine criteria, six were already solidly enforced (categories, emails, domains, titles, dedup, contact cap — zero violations found). The reseller criterion is now the strongest-verified of all: website-read, ownership-checked, independently double-checked, human-backstopped. The audit exposed two real gaps — foreign HQs and corporate ownership — affecting 39 of 553 leads, each with a designed, low-cost fix.
