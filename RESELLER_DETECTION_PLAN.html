Reseller Detection

Implementation plan — keeping reseller websites out of the lead batches
Branch feat/reseller-detection · Status: planned, pending Phase 0 measurement · 2026-06-10

1The problem (Victor's loom, June 10)

Some accepted leads are resellers — shops that sell other companies' products (a store selling Snap Circuits, where the brand on the product is Elenco, not the store) — instead of brands that make and sell their own product. Victor: "somehow we just need to make sure that they're not resellers."

Why they get through (verified against all 558 accepted leads): the current AI filter never looks at the company's website. It only sees the company name and a marketing blurb — and the blurb is useless for this (a phrase-scan "matched" 110 of 558 companies as resellers, almost all false alarms, including one whose blurb literally says it manufactures its products). BetterContact's business-type fields are 100% empty. The evidence lives on the website, so the website must be checked.

2The solution — a 5-stage funnel

Every company (not every lead — people at the same company share one verdict) flows through stages ordered cheapest-first. Each stage only sees what the previous one couldn't decide, so money and human time are spent only where genuinely needed.

FREE

Stage 0 — Group & remember

~5,000 leads ≈ ~1,700 unique companies. Each company is judged once, ever — verdicts are cached in the database, so repeat companies in future batches cost nothing.
▼
FREE

Stage 1 — Instant checks, no AI

(a) Amazon check: match against our 275k-brand SmartScout table. A registered Amazon brand needs a trademark → confirmed brand → pass. (42% of companies match — verified.)
(b) Shopify check: if the site runs Shopify, one public URL lists every product and its maker. All products their own → pass. Products from 4+ different makers → reseller → reject. (Verified: every legit brand probed showed exactly one real vendor.)
Roughly half of all companies are decided here, deterministically, for $0.
▼ undecided half
~1¢ / company

Stage 2 — Read the website + one AI call

Fetch the homepage, extract the meaningful text (title, menu, footer, page text) and hard signals — "Shop by Brand" menu? dealer language? how many other brand names? — then ask Haiku once: brand or reseller? The answer must include a confidence level and a quoted piece of evidence from the page, so every verdict is auditable. High confidence → decided. Resolves ~80% of what reaches it.
▼ low confidence, or website wouldn't load
~3¢ / company

Stage 3 — Quick web research

For the few hundred still-unclear companies, the AI does what Victor would do by hand: one web search surfacing their LinkedIn page, Amazon storefront, press mentions — then decides from those sources. Works even when the company's own site is unreachable. No browser needed (no Playwright in v1).
▼ still genuinely ambiguous
HUMAN

Stage 4 — Review queue

The last ~150–300 companies/month land in the existing Lead Reviewer page with the evidence already attached (vendor list, quoted page text, search findings) — seconds per decision instead of minutes of digging. Nothing ambiguous is ever auto-passed.

Honest limits. No filter is 100%. The residual escapes are "clean storefront" resellers that present as a single brand (these fool quick human review too), and hybrid companies that both manufacture and resell — the latter correctly land in the human queue rather than being guessed. We report a catch rate to Victor only after Phase 0 produces a measured number, not before.
Bottom line. Every scraped company gets its website (or its web footprint) actually checked before it reaches a batch — the thing the current filter never does — for tens of dollars a month, with every automated verdict carrying a quoted piece of evidence answering "why was this one accepted?"
